USMC Gear Borrowing Information:

USMC allows for it's members to borrow certain pieces of gear. You will be required to sign a liability form and demonstrate a specified level of skill which varies between each piece of gear. This site will also act as a place where you can get information about each of the climbing skills.

There is a maximum duration that you will be allowed to borrow gear for, which the club has fixed at 10 days, if gear is kept after this time you will be charged however this also means you can extend your borrow period for a fee.

Another important skill is belaying, The method that the BMC reccomends is in the Video bellow, some key points are that the teeth of the belay device should be pointing downwards, the belay device should be put onto the single vertical loop on a harness, there is an image on the side of most belay devices that will show a climber, that climber should be pointing upwards. Borrowable Gear





Sport climbing is any form of outdoor climbing where you are using bolts that have been drilled into and attached to the rock. These provide an attachment for quickdraws so that you can lead climb outdoors by clipping your rope into the quickdraws. The top of the route will then have an anchor (usually 2 bolts with a chain and a ring but this can vary) to allow you to safely descend or go on to multi-pitch another route.

It is crucial that you do not use the bolts/chains for anything other than their intended use since pulling on them can cause finger injuries and stepping on them or attaching incorrect gear can damage them. As a rule of thumb, metal gear should be attached to the bolts and the chain, whilst ropes should be run through the ring.

Clipping quickdraws

Quickdraws have two snap-gate carabiners at each end, which are differentiated since one carabiner can slide and move whilst the other is fixed. If clipped to the bolt correctly, this ensures correct loading of the bolt and quickdraw if you were to take a lead fall. It is also important to ensure correct quickdraw clipping to reduce the chances of the rope unclipping itself if you were to lead fall.

To clip correctly, the carabiner that moves (the wobbly end) should be attached to the bolt, whilst your rope is clipped into the fixed carabiner. The easiest way to remember this is always think ‘wobbly to wall’. To orientate the carabiner correctly, the gate should face away from the direction you are travelling. For example, if the next bolt is to the left of the bolt you are clipping, the quickdraw gate should face to the right (and vice versa).



Setting up top ropes/cleaning anchors

When you get to the top of a sport route, there will be an anchor and the way they look can vary a lot. The most important rules to remember are:

• You must maintain 2 points of contact at all times, meaning that you must be loaded on both bolts at all times. To do this, we use a personal anchor (sling + carabiner attached to your harness) and never fully untie the rope from ourselves.
• Its also important to never completely untie yourself from the rope in case you drop it. We also recommend trying to keep gear attached to something at all times to reduce the risks of it being dropped and lost.
• Weight bearing gear should be placed at the back of everything, including behind the chain.
• NEVER untie/detach anything that is taut and weight beating since this is likely to be what is stopping you from falling. You should only ever untie/detach something if it is loose.
• Screw-gate carabiners should always be screwed closed when in use with their gates screwed down. This allows gravity to help prevent them from accidentally unscrewing upwards.

The easiest type of anchor to use is when there are two level bolts with a chain connecting them and a ring in the middle.

To set up a top rope on this type of anchor:
1. Attach 1 quickdraw to a bolt, ensuring the gate faces outwards.
2. Clip your rope into the quickdraw.
3. Get your belayer to take and clip your personal sling to a bolt or the chain (if you are on a good ledge and feel comfortable to do so, you can omit this step).
4. Clip a 2nd quickdraw to the other bolt, ensuring the gate also faces outwards.
5. Clip yourself into the 2nd quickdraw, ensuring the rope runs smoothly through both quickdraws and the end of the rope attached to your harness comes out the front of the quickdraws. The two quickdraws should be opposite and opposed and shown in the image below.


To clean this type of anchor:
1. Attach your personal sling to a bolt or the chain.
2. Ask for slack and pull the loose rope through the ring.
3. With the loose rope, create a bite and tie a double figure of eight knot.
4. Using a screw gate carabiner, clip the double figure of eight knot to your harness’ belay loop. Ensure the screw gate is closed.
5. Get your belayer to take.
6. Untie your original rope knot. This should be easily identifiable since it will be loose since it is no longer weight bearing and will be tied through both your harness loops rather than just your belay loop.
7. Pull the loose rope through the ring (you may need some slack to do this) and loosely attach it to your harness to prevent it getting caught.
8. Remove both quickdraws from the bolts.
9. Check you are weighted on the rope and detach your personal anchor.
10. Ensure no gear has been left behind before descending.

It’s important to be able to adapt these methods to the type of anchor you are using so ensure you know what type of anchor the route has and that you have the necessary gear.

For offset anchors where the bolts aren’t level, you can use slings to alter the lengths of quickdraws/carabiners to ensure they meet at the same point so that they’re loaded equally.

For anchors without a chain (there will be 2 bolts, each with a ring directly attached to them), you must still ensure you have 2 points of contact so a rope would need to be run through both rings instead of one. You also wouldn’t be able to clip your personal anchor into the chain or rings at any point so would need to use the bolts instead.
